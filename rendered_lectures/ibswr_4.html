Question: what is the probability of the null hypothesis?

Bayes theorem again

We would like to know the probability of the hypothesis given the data: P(H0∣D)P(H0∣D)

  • using Bayes theorem

P(H0∣D)=P(D∣H0)P(H0)P(D)P(H0∣D)=P(D∣H0)P(H0)P(D)
P(D)=P(D∣H0)P(H0)+P(D∣¬H0)P(¬H0)P(D)=P(D∣H0)P(H0)+P(D∣¬H0)P(¬H0)

Need to know:

  • the prior probability of the null hypothesis: P(H0)P(H0)
  • the likelihood of the data under the null hypothesis: P(D∣H0)P(D∣H0)
  • the likelihood of the data under all other hypotheses: P(D∣¬H0)P(D∣¬H0)

Note we can rescale all likelihoods without changing P(H0∣D)P(H0∣D)

P(D∣…)→cP(D∣…)P(D∣…)→cP(D∣…)

  • can ignore constants in the likelihood

Question: how do we cope with all value of μμ?

Marginal probability

Answer: Integrate over all μμ!

First we need to define a prior probability (density) for μμ for our alternative hypothesis

  • p(μ∣¬H0)p(μ∣¬H0)

Then we use the probability rule P(A∩B)=P(A∣B)P(B)P(A∩B)=P(A∣B)P(B) to write the joint

P(D∩μ∣¬H0)=P(D∣μ,¬H0)p(μ∣¬H0)P(D∩μ∣¬H0)=P(D∣μ,¬H0)p(μ∣¬H0)

Finally we marginalise over μμ

P(D∣¬H0)=∫P(D∩μ∣¬H0)dμ=∫P(D∣μ,¬H0)p(μ∣¬H0)dμP(D∣¬H0)=∫P(D∩μ∣¬H0)dμ=∫P(D∣μ,¬H0)p(μ∣¬H0)dμ

Posterior

Rather than integrating over μμ

  • the mean μμ is the quantity we care about!

Rather than trying to find its “true” fixed value

  • how would we describe its distribution, given the information we have

p(μ∣D)∝P(D∣μ)⋅p(μ)p(μ∣D)∝P(D∣μ)⋅p(μ)

  • the posterior is proportional to the likelihood times the prior
  • follows from Bayes theorem

Summary

The posterior distribution

p(μ∣D)∝P(D∣μ)⋅p(μ)p(μ∣D)∝P(D∣μ)⋅p(μ)

  • proportional to the likelihood times the prior
  • updates prior belief with the likelihood from the data

Often we cannot compute analytically

  • obtain Monte Carlo samples from the posterior
  • MCMC, HMC etc

The credible interval contains the central 95% of the posterior

  • estimate from samples

Can also use samples to integrate/marginalise

  • eg to obtain the estimate of the probability of a hypothesis
